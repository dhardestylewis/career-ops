## Most Serious Risks
*What do you see as the most serious risks at the intersection of AI and biology, and why? (Max 350 words)*

**Draft (196 words):**
> The most serious risk is that the democratization of dual-use AI bio-design tools fundamentally shifts the bottleneck of engineered pandemics from "knowledge acquisition" to "synthesis and detection." 
> 
> Historically, engineering a pathogen required tacit wet-lab knowledge. As frontier models automate bio-design, the threat landscape shifts from a few highly monitored state actors to a massively decentralized network of potential adversaries. 
> 
> The catastrophic risk is that our current defensive infrastructure relies entirely on pathogen-specific surveillance, which cannot detect novel or engineered threats until they have already scaled within a population. If an AI agent designs a "stealth" pathogen engineered for high transmission but delayed lethality, our current spatial surveillance network will fail to detect it before geometric spread occurs.
> 
> Therefore, the true risk is an asymmetric attack on our physical urban infrastructure, exacerbated by a complete lack of real-time, pathogen-agnostic environmental monitoring. To mitigate this, we must transition from reactive medical countermeasures to proactive, continuous spatial sequencing. If we do not scale our macro-spatial biosurveillance compute infrastructure faster than AI scales biological capabilities, we face an unmitigable existential threat.

> **Paper:** *A national wastewater sequencing network to detect pandemic agents* (Esvelt et al.)
> 
> The core contribution of this proposal is the conceptualization of the Nucleic Acid Observatory (NAO)—a pathogen-agnostic biosurveillance system designed to continuously sequence global waterways. Esvelt argues that traditional pathogen-specific monitoring is structurally flawed against engineered threats. Instead, the NAO acts as a universal "tripwire" by detecting any nucleic acid exhibiting exponential growth within a population, giving society critical lead time to develop countermeasures.
> 
> This paper fundamentally shifted my view of biosecurity from a "biological" problem to a "spatial data" problem. Detecting an engineered pandemic is no longer just about virology; it is an algorithmic challenge of separating exponential signal from massive environmental noise across millions of geographic nodes. 
> 
> This realization directly aligns with my background in high-dimensional spatial ML and HPC. Esvelt's proposal relies heavily on the assumption that we can mathematically optimize sensor deployment across complex urban wastewater infrastructure and process the resulting massive metagenomic datasets in real-time. The paper convinced me that the most critical bottleneck in AIxBiosecurity is the lack of macro-spatial simulation architects capable of engineering the compute pipelines required to make the NAO operational. It crystallized my conviction that my experience scaling highly parallelized disaster-resilience models at TACC is directly applicable to defending against catastrophic biological risks.
