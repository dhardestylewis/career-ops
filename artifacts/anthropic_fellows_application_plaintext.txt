Why are you interested in participating in the Fellows program?
*
The frontier of AI capabilities is increasingly intersecting with the physical world, where the deployment of advanced models will generate massive infrastructure demands and complex societal impacts. I am applying to the Fellows program because my background bridges deep learning research in physical systems with applied engineering in spatial economics. I previously engineered the machine learning pipelines for a $40M DARPA disaster resiliency initiative at TACC, working directly with leading scientists to scale distributed ML models predicting the cascading physical failures of climate events on critical infrastructure. From there, I founded Homecastr, building an automated spatial valuation model capable of processing high-dimensional spatial-economic data at national scale, giving me firsthand experience operationalizing large-scale economic AI models.

Anthropic's rigorous, empirical approach to societal impacts aligns exactly with my methodology. Building high-stakes models at TACC taught me that intelligent systems deployed without robust systemic alignment are dangerous. The Fellows program represents the ideal environment to translate my dual background in large-scale machine learning and urban infrastructure modeling into empirical AI alignment research. By integrating Maxim Massenkoff's quantitative economic research and Deep Ganguli's systemic impact frameworks with my expertise in high-dimensional spatial modeling, I want to apply my computational foundation to forecast and shape the spatial economics and societal resilience of Anthropic's most advanced systems.

With your selected team(s) in mind, tell us briefly about one or more research areas you're excited about right now, and why.
*
I am particularly excited about the intersection of spatial economics and the Societal Impacts team's work on catastrophic systemic risk. While initiatives like Massenkoff and McCrory's Anthropic Economic Index and Huang et al.'s 81,000-user survey rigorously capture digital O*NET task exposure and qualitative sentiment, the vital next frontier is extending these empirical frameworks to predictively model AI's disruption of physical and civil infrastructure systems. Forecasting how autonomous agents might inadvertently compromise real-world constraints—such as energy grids, critical logistics, or urban supply chains—can be powerfully augmented by using Anthropic's empirical data to initialize and ground large-scale multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) simulations. Drawing on my experience scaling highly parallelized geospatial models at TACC and modeling real-world markets at Homecastr, I want to engineer the MARL simulation environments necessary to model these macro-spatial impacts, stress-testing policy interventions before deployment to ensure agentic AI remains safe and resilient within physical infrastructure.
